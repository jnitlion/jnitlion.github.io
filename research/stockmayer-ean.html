Model validation

A Coarse-Grained Stockmayer Model for Ethylammonium Nitrate

Published in The Journal of Chemical Physics 163, 044503 (2025) · with A.L. Frischknecht, M.J. Stevens, and I. Nakamura

The idea

Ionic liquids are salts that are molten at room temperature. They are attractive as safer electrolytes and green solvents, but many of them are too viscous to be practical. Atomistic simulations can predict their viscosity, but they are expensive and tied to one specific chemistry at a time. This project asks a simple question: how much chemical detail do we actually need to capture the viscosity of a real ionic liquid?

We tested the most minimal candidate, the Stockmayer fluid, in which each ion is a sphere carrying a point charge and a point dipole. Our benchmark was ethylammonium nitrate (EAN), one of the oldest and best-characterized protic ionic liquids.

What we did

We parameterized the model against EAN's molecular properties (cation diameter σ⁺ = 4.80 Å, reduced charge q = +0.35e, and dipole moment μ = 5.17 D) and ran equilibrium molecular dynamics in LAMMPS, computing viscosity with Green-Kubo stress-autocorrelation methods across a wide temperature range.

What we found

The minimal dipolar model reproduces EAN's experimental viscosity and its glass-transition behavior. This result tells us something meaningful: the transport physics of a real protic ionic liquid is controlled by a small set of molecular parameters (size, charge, and dipole moment) rather than by chemical detail. It also validates the Stockmayer framework as a fast, transferable platform for scanning ionic-liquid design space, which is exactly what the parameter-space study does next.
