Polymer dynamics

Polymerized Ionic Liquids: Viscosity Scaling, Chain Dynamics, and Ion Pairing

Manuscript in preparation (2026) · with A.L. Frischknecht, M.J. Stevens, and I. Nakamura · presented at the APS Global Physics Summit, Denver, 2026

The question

Polymerizing an ionic liquid — tethering its cations into chains — gives you a solid-like, single-ion-conducting material, attractive for safer batteries and flexible electronics. But it also raises viscosity dramatically and couples ion motion to polymer dynamics in ways that small-molecule intuition doesn't predict. How do cation size and dipole mobility control this coupling?

What we did

We extended the Stockmayer framework to polymerized ionic liquids (polyILs), running coarse-grained molecular dynamics of dipolar chains with free counterions. Across systematically varied cation sizes and dipole mobilities, we computed viscosity, ion-pair lifetimes, and chain orientational relaxation, connecting monomer-scale chemistry to melt-scale rheology.

What we found

Cation size and dipole mobility each reshape the viscosity scaling of polyILs, and they do so through distinct mechanisms: ion-pair dynamics and chain orientational relaxation respond differently to the two parameters. Together the results point to which monomer-level modifications actually buy you lower melt viscosity without sacrificing the ionic character of the material.
