On the State of Science in the Philippines

May 11, 2026 · Jes Itliong

Between 2018 and 2020, I held three jobs at once. I taught physics full-time at a public institute in Manila, covering mechanics, electromagnetism, and quantum physics for five hundred students and counting. On early-mornings, I lectured part-time at another university. In whatever hours were left, I did the thing I actually trained for: research, as an assistant running molecular dynamics simulations. Three jobs, one of which was science, and science was the one that paid least reliably.

I don't lead with this for sympathy. By Philippine standards it's an ordinary story, and I was luckier than most. I lead with it because I've lived every statistic I'm about to cite, and I want you to read the numbers the way I do. Not as abstractions, but as the arithmetic that eventually puts a scientist on a plane.

The numbers

The Philippines spends about 0.32% of its gross domestic expenditure on research and development. That's less than a third of the 1% floor UNESCO recommends for developing economies, and an order of magnitude below R&D-intensive countries like Japan at over 3%. The Department of Science and Technology's slice of the national budget hovers around half a percent. The country counts fewer than 174 full-time researchers per million people, and its innovation rankings have been sliding rather than climbing. These figures come from Desiree Hanimann's pointed 2023 essay, Undervalued and Underfunded: The State of Science and Research in the Philippines, which also contains the line I've never managed to forget: to be a scientist in the Philippines, "you must take a vow of poverty and enter a system rigged against you."

The workforce numbers are bleaker in motion than in snapshot. As Sero Toriano Parel documents in Science for the People, the number of Filipino STEM workers abroad nearly tripled between 1990 and 2010, from roughly 40,000 to 113,000. That's just one current in the broader river of thousands of Filipinos who leave the country every single day. The pipeline leaks at both ends. In the same PISA cycle Parel cites, Filipino fifteen-year-olds ranked near the bottom of 79 countries in science and mathematics, and only a fraction of a percent of students who finish secondary school ever become full-time researchers.

What the numbers feel like from inside

Statistics undercount the texture of the thing. Underfunding isn't one big dramatic deprivation. It's a thousand small ones. It's teaching loads sized so that research only happens after your fifth hour of grading. It's equipment requests that die in procurement processes so slow that, by the time approval comes, the science has already moved on. Hanimann documents that dysfunction, and every Filipino researcher can corroborate it from memory. It's contractualization, with brilliant colleagues employed semester to semester, ineligible for the stability that long-horizon research requires. It's writing grant budgets knowing that the grant, if it comes at all, may arrive after the experiment's window has already closed.

And there's the quieter cost: the sense that choosing science means choosing against your family's financial security. Nobody I trained with lacked talent or work ethic. The selection pressure runs the other way, and only the stubborn stay. What we lacked was a system that could afford to keep us.

Leaving, and what it taught me

In 2021 I became a statistic in that migration table. I left for a physics PhD at Michigan Technological University. What I found abroad was not smarter people. It was resourcing: a funded research assistantship that let science be my one job instead of my third, high-performance computing clusters I could submit to without begging, and a collaboration with a national laboratory of the kind that compounds a young scientist's growth. Within a few years I had publications in journals I'd only read about, and this year I'll defend a dissertation built on thousands of simulations that would have been physically impossible for me to run back home.

The uncomfortable lesson is that the difference between my productivity in Manila and my productivity in Michigan was never me. It was infrastructure, time, and the dignity of being paid to think. That should be an indictment of nothing except underinvestment, and a living proof of what Filipino scientists can do once the arithmetic finally works.

Declining isn't the same as doomed

I've used the word "declining," and I stand by it as a description of trajectories in budgets, rankings, and retention. But I want to resist the fatalism that usually follows. There are real bright spots. DOST scholarship programs (which I was a product of) support tens of thousands of STEM students. The Balik Scientist Program has made returning home a formal, supported path rather than a leap of faith. And a generation of Filipino researchers abroad remains intensely, sometimes achingly, connected to home. The raw material of talent, drive, and a culture that reveres education has never been the missing ingredient.

The fixes are known and boringly structural, which is exactly why both articles I've cited converge on the same list: fund R&D toward the 1% floor, fix procurement, end the contractualization of research staff, and pay scientists as if their staying mattered. None of this is mysterious. It's a choice, renewed or declined every budget cycle.

What I owe

I think often about what someone in my position owes the system that produced him, because it did produce me: public schools, a public institute, and teachers who taught five sections and still stayed late. My answers so far are modest. I mentor Filipino students who want to go into research, and I try to be honest with them about both the costs and the routes. I build my tools in the open, as simulations and dashboards that run in a browser, because free instruments are the ones that reach students at institutions like the one that trained me. And someday, in whatever form the arithmetic allows, I intend for the knowledge to make the return trip.

The state of science in the Philippines is not a verdict on Filipino scientists. It's a bill that keeps coming due. My generation of the diaspora is still deciding whether it gets paid, and every one of us who left is, in some corner of the heart, still doing the math.
