The EV Hype Is Real. The Electrolyte Is the Bottleneck.

July 2, 2026 · Jes Itliong

Electric vehicles are having the loudest moment any technology has had since the smartphone, and the numbers behind the noise are genuinely impressive. According to the IEA's Global EV Outlook, electric car sales passed 17 million in 2024, which is more than one in five new cars sold worldwide, and the number keeps climbing. Governments have bet industrial policy on them, and automakers have bet their futures.

I'm not here to argue against the hype. Electrifying transport is one of the few decarbonization levers that is actually being pulled at scale. My argument is that the public conversation is fixated on the wrong end of the car. We talk about range, charging networks, and sticker prices. Meanwhile the thing that quietly limits all three, and decides whether your battery is safe, cheap, and durable, is a component most people have never thought about: the electrolyte.

The unglamorous heart of the battery

A lithium-ion battery is, functionally, two electrodes arguing over lithium ions, with the electrolyte as the medium of negotiation. Every property you care about at the showroom traces back to it. Fast charging? That's ionic transport through the electrolyte. Cold-weather range loss? Electrolyte viscosity rising as temperature drops. The rare but headline-grabbing battery fire? Today's commercial electrolytes are lithium salts dissolved in flammable organic carbonates, which are excellent conductors and, unfortunately, excellent fuel. The battery problem has always been, in large part, an electrolyte problem. Armand and Tarascon were making versions of this point in their landmark Building better batteries back in 2008.

The contenders

Solid-state electrolytes are the media darlings. Replace the flammable liquid with a ceramic or sulfide solid, enable lithium-metal anodes, and energy density jumps while fire risk plummets. The physics progress is real, and some solid conductors now rival liquid-electrolyte conductivities. But as Janek and Zeier carefully argue in their assessment of the solid-state roadmap, the hard problems are interfacial: maintaining intimate solid-to-solid contact through thousands of charge cycles, suppressing dendrites that find every grain boundary, and manufacturing brittle ceramics at automotive scale and cost. Solid-state batteries have been "about five years away" for over a decade now. I believe they'll arrive. I've just stopped predicting when.

Ionic liquids, which are molten salts at room temperature, are the chemist's elegant answer: essentially non-volatile, non-flammable, and electrochemically robust. Comprehensive reviews by Watanabe and co-workers and MacFarlane and co-workers lay out their promise across energy storage. So why isn't your car full of them? In one word: viscosity. Most ionic liquids are honey where a battery needs water, and a viscous electrolyte means sluggish ions, slow charging, and poor low-temperature performance. This is not a side detail to me. It is literally my dissertation. Which molecular features make an ionic liquid viscous, and which knobs can we turn to thin it out without losing its virtues? That's the question my parameter-space work tries to answer at the level of design rules, rather than one compound at a time.

Polymerized ionic liquids push the idea further. Tether the ions into a polymer and you get a material that handles like a plastic film, conducts only the ion you want moving (a "single-ion conductor," so no concentration gradients sap your power), and cannot leak or burn like a liquid. The field, mapped in reviews like Yuan, Mecerreyes, and Antonietti's, sits right at the intersection of safety and processability that EV batteries want. The catch is the same tax paid twice: polymerization raises viscosity and slows ion transport even further. Working out how chain connectivity, ion size, and dipole mobility set that penalty, and where it can be evaded, is the other half of my dissertation.

Hype as a demand signal

Here's the synthesis I'd offer. The EV hype isn't wrong. It's early, in a specific sense: the market demand has arrived ahead of the materials that would fully satisfy it. Every overpromise about 10-minute charging or fireproof packs is, in effect, a requisition order sent to materials physics, and most of those orders route through the electrolyte. That's genuinely good news for my field. It's rare for fundamental soft-matter physics (why is this fluid viscous? how do ions move through a charged polymer?) to map so directly onto something the world is this impatient for.

So when the discourse tires of counting charging stations, I'd ask it to spare a thought for the negotiation medium: the unglamorous, flammable, viscous, and genuinely fascinating liquid in the middle of every cell. The cars are here. The electrolyte they deserve is still being designed, molecule by molecule. Some of us are on it.
