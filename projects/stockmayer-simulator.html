What you're looking at

Each sphere is a coarse-grained ion: a Lennard-Jones bead (size σ, attraction strength ε) carrying a point dipole (moment μ, drawn as an arrow). This Stockmayer fluid is the minimal model my research uses to ask which molecular properties control the viscosity of ionic liquids — the same framework that quantitatively reproduces the viscosity of ethylammonium nitrate. All quantities are in reduced (dimensionless) units, the standard convention in molecular simulation.

Things to try

  • Crank μ up to 2+ — dipoles attract head-to-tail, and you'll see particles line up into chains and rings. This self-association is a key reason polar liquids are viscous.
  • Drop the temperature below ~0.3 — motion becomes sluggish and structure freezes in, a glimpse of glassy dynamics.
  • Turn on NPT and raise the pressure — the box compresses until the fluid pushes back. Lower it and the system expands toward a gas.
  • Set μ = 0 — you recover a plain Lennard-Jones fluid; compare how much livelier it is at the same temperature.

What this demo simplifies

This is a teaching demonstration, not a research instrument. To run in real time it uses a small system (periodic boundaries), truncated dipole–dipole interactions instead of Ewald summation, Berendsen-style thermostat and barostat, and gentle force caps for numerical stability. My research simulations use LAMMPS with long-range electrostatics, rigorous ensembles, and thousands of particles — but the qualitative physics you see here (dipolar chaining, glassy slowdown, pressure–density coupling) is the real thing.

Built with plain JavaScript and Three.js; the physics engine is ~200 lines of velocity-Verlet MD you can read in this page's source.
