Don't Get Glassy

A liquid that cools too fast to crystallize doesn't freeze. Instead it vitrifies: molecules slow down until they're trapped mid-disorder. That's the glass transition, and tonight it's coming for your simulation box.

Hold mouse / touch to heat the region under your cursor. Keep the liquid liquid. When ~85% freezes, it's over.

The physics you're fighting

Cool a liquid slowly and it usually crystallizes: molecules find their ordered, low-energy arrangement. Cool it fast enough and they don't get the chance. The liquid's own sluggishness outruns its search for order, relaxation times explode from picoseconds toward centuries, and the material solidifies while still structurally a liquid. That kinetic arrest is the glass transition, and the temperature where viscosity reaches a staggering 1012 Pa·s is called Tg. It's one of the deepest unsolved problems in condensed matter physics, and it's the central character of my dissertation, where we extract Tg from simulated viscosity curves of ionic liquids.

Two honest details are baked into the game. First, the particles come in two sizes: a one-size liquid in 2D crystallizes almost instantly on cooling, so simulators use bidisperse mixtures to frustrate crystallization, the same trick used in real glass-transition research. Second, your inevitable defeat is physically faithful. At fixed density, with the thermostat marching downward, vitrification isn't a puzzle you can solve, only one you can postpone. The game's real question is the same one my research asks: what controls how long you can keep a cooling liquid mobile?

What this game simplifies

The "molecules" are soft repulsive disks with Langevin dynamics: no attractions, no chemistry, and a local "frozen" label based on each particle's recent mobility rather than a proper relaxation-function analysis. Your heat gun is a cheerful fiction (real experimentalists cannot locally reheat a quenching melt with a crosshair, much as they might wish to). And the cooling rates here are, by molecular standards, apocalyptic. For the real thing, with VFT fits, fragility, and Tg from transport, see the research section.
