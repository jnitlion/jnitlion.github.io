The thought experiment you're playing

In 1867, James Clerk Maxwell imagined a "very observant and neat-fingered being" guarding a tiny door between two chambers of gas. By letting only fast molecules pass one way and slow ones the other, the demon could make one side hot and the other cold without doing any work, seemingly violating the Second Law of Thermodynamics, the rule that entropy never decreases in an isolated system. The puzzle bothered physicists for over a century.

The modern resolution is one of the most beautiful ideas in physics: information is physical. To sort molecules, the demon must measure them and remember the results, and erasing that memory has an unavoidable thermodynamic cost (Landauer's principle: at least kBT ln 2 of heat per bit). Add up the demon's bookkeeping and the Second Law survives, every time. Your brain, watching particles and timing the gate, is paying that entropy bill right now. The sorting you achieve in the box is more than paid for by the physics of you achieving it.

What this game simplifies

The gas here is ideal: molecules fly ballistically, bounce elastically off the walls, and don't collide with each other. This is why each molecule keeps its speed, and why "hot" and "cold" are permanent labels rather than a distribution that re-thermalizes. Real gases would re-mix their velocities through collisions, making the demon's job harder still. Temperatures shown are in reduced units (mean kinetic energy per molecule per chamber), and the entropy meter tracks mixing entropy only. The demon's own measurement costs are, mercifully for your score, not billed.

Built with plain JavaScript and a <canvas>: no frameworks, no build step, and a physics loop you can read in this page's source. Companion piece to the Stockmayer fluid simulator.
